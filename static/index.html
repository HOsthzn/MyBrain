💡 How to Actually Remember What You Learn
Why this lives here — not in NotebookLM
🧠 Your vault is your single source of truth
NotebookLM generates quizzes and flashcards from raw, scattered sources — PDFs, articles, YouTube transcripts. The output is only as clean as the input.

My Brain first distills all of that into validated, deduplicated, interlinked notes in 01 - Knowledge/. When you generate flashcards or a quiz here, they're built from that clean foundation — no contradictions, no noise, no repeated concepts from five different sources saying the same thing differently.

The result: review materials that are accurate, stable, and consistent with exactly what you've validated and chosen to keep.
The science — ranked by evidence
Decades of cognitive science agree: most people study in the least effective way possible. Re-reading notes feels productive but barely moves the needle. Here's what actually works, ranked by long-term retention power.
⚡ 1. Active Recall — the king
Force yourself to retrieve information from memory without looking at your notes. The act of struggling to remember is what builds the memory trace — not the looking up.
→ This is exactly what flashcards do. Close the answer, think hard, then flip.
📅 2. Spaced Repetition — the multiplier
Review the same material at increasing intervals: today → tomorrow → 3 days → 1 week → 2 weeks → 1 month. Each review right before you'd forget re-anchors it for longer. Combined with active recall, this is the closest thing to a cheat code for long-term memory.
→ Install the Obsidian Spaced Repetition plugin — your flashcard files are already formatted for it.
🗣 3. Feynman Technique — for deep understanding
Explain the concept out loud as if teaching it to someone who's never heard of it. When you hit jargon, hand-waves, or gaps — that's exactly where your understanding breaks down. Go fix those spots.
→ After a flashcard session, pick the 3 cards you found hardest and explain them out loud.
🔀 4. Interleaving — mix it up
Don't study one topic in one block. Mix different topics or subtopics in a single session. It feels harder and more confusing — that difficulty is the learning. It forces your brain to discriminate between concepts and strengthens transfer.
→ Generate quizzes across multiple topics in one sitting instead of one topic per day.

Recommended flow in My Brain

Learn a topic — use the Learn tab to run the full pipeline. NotebookLM researches, you validate and move notes to Knowledge.
2
Generate materials — come here, select the topic, generate Flashcards + Mind Map. Takes 30–60 seconds.
3
Same day — do a full flashcard session. Don't skip this — first review within 24 hours is the most important.
4
Day 3, Day 7, Day 14 — run the Quiz to test yourself under pressure. Check the Mind Map to see connections you may have missed.
5
Monthly — a quick flashcard pass keeps it in long-term memory indefinitely. The Obsidian Spaced Repetition plugin automates this scheduling for you.
Bottom line
If you only do one thing: after every flashcard session, pick the 3 hardest cards and explain them out loud as if teaching someone. You'll immediately know which gaps to revisit in your vault. That single habit — active recall + immediate Feynman check — will put your retention in the top 5% of learners.
